Problem
The gap in the experience

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit. Tabletop RPG players currently rely on a patchwork of tools — notes apps, Discord messages, physical notebooks, PDFs — to track the sprawling details of a campaign. The friction of switching between systems during play breaks immersion and creates real information loss.

This project asked: what would a companion tool look like if it was designed from the ground up for how players actually play — messy, spontaneous, and story-first?

Research
Understanding the player

Conducted 6 semi-structured interviews with TTRPG players ranging from casual to experienced. Ran a competitive audit of 8 existing tools. Key insight: players didn't want another system to maintain — they wanted something that stayed out of the way and surfaced the right information at the right moment.

Affinity map / research synthesis
Process
From flows to frames
01
User flows & IA
Mapped 4 primary user flows: starting a session, logging a quest update, searching past notes, and ending a session with a summary. Hierarchy was built around the session as the primary unit.
02
Low-fi wireframes
Three rounds of lo-fi wireframing, each tested with 2 participants. Major finding: the "quest log" metaphor wasn't landing — players thought in terms of NPCs and events, not missions.
03
Usability testing (Maze)
Remote usability test with 8 participants on mid-fi prototype. 73% task completion rate on first run. Iterated navigation and entry points based on drop-off data.
04
Hi-fi & handoff
Produced hi-fi prototype in Figma with a custom design system: dark mode-first, accessible contrast ratios, and a typographic system inspired by fantasy game UIs without being clichéd.
Final Design

Reflection
What I took away

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit. This project pushed me to design for a specific subculture — not a generic "user" — which meant the research had to be real and the design had to earn trust. The biggest lesson: the best game UI disappears. It gives you exactly what you need exactly when you need it, then gets out of the way.

Concept
The design premise

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit. Verdant is a 2D puzzle-platformer set in a world reclaimed by nature, where a small robot must navigate ruins using a light mechanic — casting light reveals paths, activates ancient machinery, and frightens away plant-creatures that block the way.

The core design constraint: every puzzle must be solvable through observation alone. No tutorials, no hint systems — the environment teaches the rules through layered environmental storytelling.

Process
Designing the levels
01
Mechanic definition
Defined 4 light mechanic interactions before designing a single room. Each interaction was documented with expected player behavior and intended emotional beat.
02
Bubble diagrams → tile maps
Sketched each level as a bubble diagram (spaces and connections) before translating to grid. This kept the spatial logic clean before visual noise was added.
03
Playtesting (3 rounds)
Watched 8 participants attempt Level 1. Collected think-aloud data. Key failure: players didn't realize the plant-creatures were afraid of light — added visual flinch animation to telegraph the rule.
04
Difficulty tuning & GDD finalization
Adjusted puzzle complexity curves based on playtest data. Wrote full GDD including narrative overview, mechanic reference, level briefs, and design rationale for each room.
Level Maps

Reflection
What I learned

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet. Designing levels taught me that a good puzzle isn't clever — it's clear. The "aha" moment only works if the player can see the pieces; the designer's job is arranging them so the solution feels inevitable in hindsight. The hardest part was resisting the urge to add complexity when simplicity was working.

Concept
Form as material conversation

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit. The brief asked for a consumer electronics product that used an unexpected material. Ceramic was the choice — not just as surface treatment, but as a genuine structural material that would influence the product's proportions, weight, and texture in ways plastic or aluminum couldn't.

The design challenge: ceramic forms are organic and irregular; speaker acoustics require precise geometries. The tension between those two requirements became the concept itself.

Development
Sketch to render
01
Reference & material research
Studied ceramic production techniques, acoustic enclosure geometry, and existing soft-goods consumer electronics (Sonos, Bang & Olufsen, Transparent). Identified the design language of "quiet confidence."
02
Thumbnail sketches (30+)
Explored a wide range of forms — cylindrical, vessel-like, stacked, asymmetric. Narrowed to three directions through peer review. Selected the "stacked vessel" form for its material honesty.
03
3D modeling in Blender
Built the form using subdivision surface modeling to preserve organic curves. Detailed the speaker grille, control interface, and cable routing while maintaining the handmade silhouette.
04
Rendering & material study
Developed two colorway options (natural stoneware, matte black) with brass accent hardware. Rendered across studio, lifestyle, and detail views.

Reflection
What I took away

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet. This was my first project where the material genuinely dictated the form rather than the other way around. Working in Blender pushed me to think about volume and weight in a way flat design doesn't — every decision about curve radius had an implication for manufacturing, structural integrity, and how it would feel in someone's hands.

Brief
Bringing a brand to life in time

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit. The project brief: design a complete motion identity for a fictional creative studio. The identity had to work across a 15-second logo ident, a 3-second social bumper, and a set of reusable broadcast components — all expressing the same personality while functioning independently.

The fictional studio concept: a boutique design firm known for typography-led work with a dry, intellectual edge. The motion language had to reflect that.

Process
Building the motion language
01
Motion moodboard
Referenced editorial animation, title sequences (Saul Bass, Kyle Cooper), and kinetic typography work. Identified the studio's motion vocabulary: deliberate, unhurried, typographically confident.
02
Storyboarding
Storyboarded the 15-second ident in 12 frames before opening After Effects. Defined the easing approach (custom bezier, never ease-in-out defaults) and the grid system the animation would follow.
03
After Effects build
Built the ident using shape layers and text animators, no stock effects. Created a master composition with swappable elements so components could be updated globally.
04
Component system
Adapted the core animation into the bumper, lower thirds, and title card system. Each component shares the same easing and timing DNA while working independently.
Final Work

Reflection
What I learned

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet. Motion design is timing more than anything else — and timing is surprisingly hard to articulate. The gap between "almost right" and "exactly right" is a matter of 3 frames and a bezier handle. This project taught me to slow down and listen to animation the way you listen to music: feel the beat before you adjust the graph editor.

Overview

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet. Bloom Loop is a social-first animation designed around the seamless loop — where the last frame is identical to the first so the animation plays infinitely without a visible reset. The floral forms use After Effects expressions to drive their timing, meaning the animation is generative rather than hand-keyframed at every point. The piece was designed for Instagram at 1:1 and 9:16 aspect ratios.
